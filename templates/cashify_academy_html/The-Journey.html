The Journey Called Life

How to Live, Not Just Survive: Uncovering Your Purpose

  • Admin
  • May 12, 2020
  • 10 Comments

In the grand tapestry of existence, every creation, no matter how insignificant it may seem, serves a purpose. The mobile device in your hand facilitates communication and learning. A pen transforms thoughts into reality. Similarly, you, too, have a unique purpose—a reason for being. However, many of us forget this truth, often buried beneath societal expectations and the relentless pursuit of achievements like degrees or job titles.

But pause for a moment. Is money, status, or accolades truly the purpose of life? These elements, while important, are merely tools to enhance our experience, not define it. The real purpose of life lies in something deeper, something eternal, something meaningful not just for ourselves but for others.

Reevaluating the Pursuit of Happiness

Many people mistakenly equate money with happiness, yet history and personal experiences repeatedly disprove this notion. Consider those on their deathbeds; they don’t ask to see their money, trophies, or degrees. Instead, they seek the presence of loved ones, moments of connection, and the warmth of shared memories.

The misconception of "money equals happiness" is further revealed in the daily grind. If you work a typical 9-to-5 job, earning a six-figure salary, and sleep from 10 PM to 8 AM, you’re left with approximately 5 hours a day for personal time. But even that time isn’t entirely yours—you need to eat, bathe, and manage other daily tasks. This begs the question: is selling your life in increments of $25 per hour truly worth it? Money can’t buy time, and once spent, time is irreplaceable.

So why do we idolize wealth? Perhaps it’s because society often confuses wealth accumulation with fulfillment. But true fulfillment comes from purpose—a sense of contributing to something greater than yourself.


Finding Purpose: Lessons from Humanity’s Innovators

When we look at some of the world’s most influential figures, we see a pattern. They are not merely chasing wealth but striving to leave an impact. Elon Musk, for instance, isn’t content with being one of the richest individuals alive. Instead, he channels his resources and energy into innovations like renewable energy, space exploration, and sustainable living—endeavors aimed at advancing humanity.

Similarly, countless others dedicate their lives to bettering the human experience. Educators who inspire, scientists who innovate, and social workers who heal remind us that true purpose is rooted in service to others. This is the essence of humanity: building, evolving, and uplifting one another.


The Japanese Philosophy of Ikigai

Japan offers a profound framework for understanding life’s purpose: Ikigai. Translating loosely to “reason for being,” Ikigai combines four elements:

1. What you love (Passion)

This is the area of things you deeply enjoy. It could be a hobby or activity that excites you and brings you joy, regardless of external factors like money.

Example:A person who loves playing the piano. They play for the joy of music, not for fame or profit.

2. What you're good at (Profession):

These are the skills, talents, or strengths that come naturally to you or have been developed over time through practice.


Example:Someone who has honed their skills as a graphic designer, able to create beautiful and functional designs with ease.

3. What the world needs (Mission):

This relates to contributing to something bigger than yourself. It's about using your passion and skills to serve others or to meet a need in the world.


Example:A teacher who imparts knowledge to students, helping them grow and contribute to society. The world needs educated individuals, and the teacher plays a vital role in that process.

4. What you can be paid for (Vocation):

This is where you consider how to turn your passion and skills into a way to make a living. It's not just about doing something for free; it's about receiving compensation for your work.


Example:A software engineer who is paid to build apps. Their coding skills (profession) are aligned with a market need (vocation) and bring value to society (mission).

Ikigai is about finding purpose that blends joy, contribution, and practicality. It encourages individuals live meaningfully, not just by earning money, but by doing something that makes them feel valuable to others while nurturing personal fulfillment.


Purpose Through the Lens of the Quran

The Quran provides divine guidance on life’s purpose. In Surah Adh-Dhariyat (51:56), Allah says:

> "And I did not create the jinn and mankind except to worship Me."
This worship isn’t confined to rituals alone but extends to living a life of righteousness, compassion, and contribution to society. Acts of kindness, justice, and service are forms of worship that fulfill our divine purpose.

Moreover, the Quran emphasizes balance and community:

> “And cooperate in righteousness and piety, but do not cooperate in sin and aggression.” (Surah Al-Ma'idah, 5:2)

Our purpose, therefore, must align with the greater good—not just personal benefit but collective harmony and progress.


What Makes a Purposeful Life?

A purposeful life transcends selfish pursuits. It’s not just about what we achieve but how our achievements influence others. Imagine a world where everyone’s goals included making life better for others. Innovations, kindness, and progress would become natural byproducts of such an approach.

Here are a few steps to uncover and live your purpose:

1. Reflect on Your Talents

What are you naturally good at? Your skills are clues to your purpose.

2. Identify Your Passions

What activities make you lose track of time? These are often aligned with what you love.

3. Understand the World’s Needs

How can your talents and passions solve a problem or fulfill a need?

4. Seek Divine Guidance

Pray, meditate, or study scriptures to gain clarity and direction.


A Legacy of Service

In the end, our purpose isn’t just about self-discovery—it’s about service. It’s about asking, “How can I make the world a better place?” Whether through small acts of kindness or groundbreaking innovations, every contribution matters.

As humans, we are architects of society. If we see flaws—bullying, corruption, inequality—it is our responsibility to address them. Just as we update technology, we must also update humanity by fostering compassion, integrity, and progress.


Final Thoughts

The journey called life is about more than just surviving. It’s about living with intention, pursuing a purpose that benefits not just you but those around you. As the Quran, Ikigai, and the lives of impactful individuals teach us, true fulfillment comes from aligning personal goals with collective well-being.

So, take a step back. Reflect. Discover your purpose. And live not just for yourself, but for a better world.
